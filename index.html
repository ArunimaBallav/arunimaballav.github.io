INTRODUCTION

The human hand is a marvel of anatomical complexity, comprising bones, muscles, tendons, and nerves. With 27 bones, including eight carpal bones in the wrist, five metacarpal bones in the palm, and fourteen phalanges in the fingers, the hand allows us to perform a wide range of tasks, from the most basic to the highly intricate. Muscles controlling the hand and fingers are located in the forearm and palm, connected to hand bones by tendons. These muscles, along with the intricate network of nerves originating from the brachial plexus, play a vital role in enabling grip strength, dexterity, and muscle strength, all of which are integral components of our daily activities. Grip dexterity, supported by forearm and wrist muscles, is crucial for numerous daily activities, including writing, typing, grasping objects, and performing fine motor skills. However, various diseases can lead to a loss of grip dexterity and muscle function, including Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS), Muscular Dystrophy, Peripheral Neuropathy, Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis), Sarcopenia, and Frailty. Understanding the importance of muscle strength, grip strength, and dexterity is essential for maintaining independence, efficiency, and preventing accidents or injuries in daily life. In this article, we delve deeper into the anatomy of the hand, exploring the crucial role of these components and their significance in our daily lives. We also discuss diseases related to the loss of grip dexterity and muscle function, as well as the importance of muscle strength, grip strength, and dexterity for healthy aging and overall well-being. Finally, we examine the muscles and bones involved in grip strength, providing insights into the complex interplay of these essential physical attributes.

Motivation

Assessing grip strength, dexterity, and muscle strength is crucial for early detection of age-related decline and neurological disorders. Understanding these metrics helps healthcare professionals mitigate the risk of injuries, improve outcomes, and enhance quality of life. It also informs the design of tools and workspaces, reduces work-related injuries, and enhances productivity. Moreover, it aids in optimizing sports performance, minimizing the risk of overuse injuries, and developing effective rehabilitation strategies.

Aim and Objectives

AIM

To establish relation between muscle activity, hand grip and hand dexterity in adult individuals with and without sports background.

OBJECTIVE

1. To measure and compare hand grip force by using a dynamometer with two hand dexterity task values.

2. To obtain data from the adult population in the age group of 18 years to 30 years.

3. To design a single lead EMG and analyze relationship between muscle strength and dexterity for sports population.

4. To Perform statistical correlation analysis of data to establish relation between grip strength, dexterity and muscle activity.

Result

Grip Strength (kg): The mean grip strength in the second study (28.976 kg) is higher than in the first study (22.85 kg). The grip strength in the second study also has a wider range (from 18.77 kg to 53.09 kg) compared to the first study (from 10.12 kg to 30 kg). This suggests that the participants in the second study might have, on average, stronger grip strength and a wider variation in grip strength compared to those in the first study. Time for Gripping (Sec): The mean time for gripping is similar in both studies, around 6.6 seconds, with very little variation. Dexterity Mistake Count: The mean dexterity mistake count is lower in the second study (20.05) compared to the first study (24.65). The median dexterity mistake count is also lower in the second study (17) compared to the first study (17). Time to Complete the Test: The mean time to complete the test is slightly higher in the second study (9.29 seconds) compared to the first study (9.25 seconds). The variation in completion time is slightly lower in the second study compared to the first study. EMG CH 1 Peak Value (Volt): The mean peak EMG value is lower in the second study (198.95 Volt) compared to the first study (239.4 Volt). The variability in peak EMG value is higher in the second study compared to the first study. The Highest Time Val (Sec): The mean highest time value is slightly higher in the second study (4.795 seconds) compared to the first study (4.55 seconds). The variability in the highest time value is lower in the second study compared to the first study. Overall, the second study appears to have participants with stronger grip strength, fewer dexterity mistakes, and lower peak EMG values compared to the first study. However, the time taken to complete the test is slightly longer in the second study. Conclusion

The comparative analysis between participants hailing from sports and non-sports backgrounds illuminates intriguing insights into physical capabilities and motor skills. Despite initial expectations of potentially divergent outcomes, the examination reveals striking similarities amidst subtle differences, offering a nuanced understanding of human performance. Firstly, the evaluation of grip strength, a fundamental indicator of upper limb functionality, unveils a consistent narrative across both groups. Regardless of sporting engagement, male and female participants exhibit akin average grip strength levels, highlighting a shared baseline of physical prowess. Moreover, the moderate variability and wide range witnessed in grip strength underscore the multifaceted nature of this attribute, transcending the boundaries of sporting affiliation.

Future Enhancement

Longitudinal Studies: Conducting longitudinal studies to track changes in grip strength, muscle activity, and dexterity performance over time among individuals from sports and non-sports backgrounds. Comparative Analysis: Comparing the impact of different types of sports or physical activities on hand health and performance. In-depth Mechanistic Studies: Delving deeper into the underlying mechanisms that drive the observed correlations between grip strength, muscle activity, and dexterity performance. Intervention Development: Designing and testing interventions aimed at improving hand health and performance in populations from both sports and non-sports backgrounds. Inclusion of Additional Variables: Exploring the influence of additional variables, such as age, gender, occupation, and hand dominance, on hand health and performance.
